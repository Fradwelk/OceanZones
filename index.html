MARINE ANIMALS BY DEPTH

The ocean makes up 70% of the entire world and contains most of the life found in our biosphere. The ocean contains 5 main zones, The Sunlight Zone (Epipelagic), The Twilight Zone (Mesopelagic), The Midnight Zone (Bathypelagic), The Abyssal Zone (Abyssopelagic) and The Hadal Zone (Trenches). Here we will see what sea creatures we can find lurking in these depths.

The Sunlight Zone (Surface - 200m)

This is the top most layer of the ocean, most aquatic plants and animals are found in this region. Here, the sunlight is still able to penerate the environment, meaning photosynthesis can occur.

Most predators can be found in this zone, since most of their prey is found here. Take sharks for example.

Filter feeders like manta rays are found in this zone, where they spend their time feeding on plankton.

Animals like tuna can also be found here, they are often seen in very large groups such as in the picture above.

The Twilight Zone (200m - 1,000m)

In this zone, the sun received from the surface is little to none. Animals at this depth are either blind or bioluminescent in order to cope with the lack of light.Some animals migrate between the Sunlight zone and Twilight zone.

The cuttlefish, a camouflaging invertebrate can be seen lurking in these depths.

This terrifying creature is called the viperfish, it uses its lower jaw to trap prey in its mouth, making it a swimming death trap.

One example of aquatic animals who migrate to and from the Twilight zone is the lanternfish. They spend the day hiding from predators in the Twilight zone and return to the Sunlight zone at night.

The Midnight Zone (1,000m - 4,000m)

This zone is in almost complete darkness. Aside from the lack of light, organisms here must also be able to deal with near freezing temperatures at around 4 degrees celsius.

Sea vents like these provide heat and nutrients for micro-organisms to survive down in these depths.

The giant sea squid is one of the most infamous residents of this zone.

While not a permanent inhabitant of this zone, the sperm whale frequently reaches these depths during its dives. This is also where the giant squid and sperm whale face-off in battles which leaves permanent scars on the whale.

The Abyssal Zone (4,000m - 11,000m)

At these depths it is extremely hard for any life to survive. Not only do they have to deal with the freezing temperature, but the pressure in this zone is that of 200 - 600 atmospheres.

Possibly the face of deep-sea creatures, the anglerfish lures in prey with the light on their head and quickly devours them.

Bioluminescence is quite common in this zone. Since there is no light down here, animals have to create their own. One example is this glowing jellyfish.

The cookie cutter shark, which takes small cookie shaped bites out of its prey, was discovered after it took a bite out of a submarine.

The Hadal Zone (Bottom of the ocean)

This is the deepest and most extreme point in the ocean. The pressure and temperature in this zone makes it almost impossible for organisms to survive, but as they say, life finds a way.

The Hirondellea gigas is one of the few known residents of the Trenches at the bottom of the ocean. It is nicknamed the deep sea shrimp.

The snailfish can be found anywhere in the ocean, from the surface all the way to the barren wasteland that is the Marianna Trench.

Most of the Hadal zone is still very much unexplored, we have only been there a handful of times and we know very little. We have no idea what other creatures could be lurking in these deep dark waters.
